Hearing the Grass Grow gathers articles, interviews, and reflections on artists and artworks that explore the posthuman, questioning what it means to be human in relation to technology, the environment and non-human life. Inspired by the words of George Eliot on the front cover, reinterpreted through Rosi Braidotti’s contemporary theory, the book invites readers to “listen differently”, to perceive what usually goes unnoticed or silent. The book highlights artistic practices across media, from biotech experiments to immersive digital spaces, that make posthuman experiences visible, showing alternative ways of sensing, connecting, and existing. Acting as a reflective pause, it traces the development of posthuman ideas from the 1990s to today, offering insights and prompts rather than definitive answers. At its core, the project emphasizes art’s power to reveal hidden worlds, to listen beyond the human, and to rethink our place within a deeply interconnected, more-than-human reality.

In a 1976 interview, italian author Achille Campanile revealed that many of his works were originally conceived for the stage but, due to financial reasons, eventually took the form of novels. This edition embraces that original intent and brings it to life, guiding the reader to the boundary between the written page and theatrical performance. The prose text has been transformed into a script and, between acts, just as in the theater, when the lights come up, the audience is momentarily pulled away from the performance they were immersed in. The dream is interrupted, and their attention returns to the surrounding reality: the theater hall, the other spectators, their gestures, expressions, and behaviors. In this pause, I have created a parallel narrative, a second layer of storytelling that unfolds through a series of photographic sequences.

An immersive installation project developed as part of a stage design exercise, imagined for the 2025 Triennale Milano International Exhibition on the theme of inequality. The exhibition is inspired by the work of artist JR and reinterprets some of his most iconic projects (Déplacés, Women Are Heroes, Migrants Picnic Across a Border) within a seven-room exhibition path, each space featuring strong sensory and symbolic elements. The concept is expressed through experiential environments built with evocative materials: suspended fabrics, translucent walls, sand, and monumental-scale images, that guide visitors through an immersive and participatory journey. The installation aims to turn the visit into an emotional and collective experience, in keeping with the artist’s visual and social approach.

The brief required the design of a collaboration between Nespresso and Mudec, the Museum of Cultures in Milan, translated into three packaging designs, a shopper, a poster, three Instagram posts, and three Instagram stories. My goal was to visually interpret the museum’s mission by creatively representing the intersection of the main cultures featured in its collection: African, Asian, and Latin American. Inspired by the patterns and decorative motifs of the artifacts on display, I developed three distinct designs, each reflecting the forms and colors of a specific culture. Each variant is associated with a representative tribe or ethnic group, along with a corresponding name for the packaging and capsule.
